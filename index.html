Battery, thermal runaway

I designed a very basic battery pack just to show a few outputs that can be obtained from simplified thermal runaway simulations. All data inputs can be easily found in the literature.

This battery pack consists of the following

At module level:
  • 12 pouch cells (6S2P configuration)
  • Internal busbars
  • Endplates
  • Busbar frames
  • Insulation layers
  • Mica sheets
  • Module's case
At pack level:
  • Modules (8)
  • HV busbars
  • Structural components
  • Thermal systems (only cooling plates and lines)
  • Valves/degassing units (4)


1. Battery Gas Venting

  • Generally, inputs come from 1D simulation and heating pad tests, and represent the mass flow rate (MFR) released by a single cell (or even multiple cells) in a thermal runaway (TR) event.
  • The MFR profile used here corresponds to a very relaxed scenario (usually it can be 5 times bigger in magnitude).


Some outputs from these types of simulations include:

  • Time it takes for the valves to open and how long it takes for the pack to be filled with vent gas.
  • Preferable path of the hot vent gas.
  • Gas temperature @ the valves.
  • Gauge pressure of the packâ€™s air gap and location of high-pressure spots on the top cover of the pack.
  • Approximation of the sealing temperature and location of hot spots.
  • Location of the module that receives the highest thermal load.
  • With these outputs, it is possible to add mica sheets/insulation layers (on modules and busbars), deflectors, thermal barriers, etc., where needed.
  • These results serve as inputs for module-to-module thermal propagation (M2M TP) (to predict the time it takes for the non-event module to go into TR) and for the vehicle team.


2. Module-to-module thermal propagation

  • Inputs for this case usually come from ARC tests (maximum energy released, self-heating and short-circuit triggering temperatures) and gas venting simulations
  • The main output of M2M TP is the time it takes for the non-event module (NEM) to go into thermal runaway.
  • For this pack, I considered four cases: case 1, which does not have mica sheets, insulation layers or coolant, case 2, which does have the three aforementioned things (but with static coolant), case 3, which is the same as case 2 but with coolant flowing (inlet @ 5 lpm), and case 4 which is the same as the previous case but @ 10 lpm.


Observations from this example:

  • Conduction via the cooling plate is the mode of heat transfer that predominates (77%), while convection due to vent gas is the second one (20%).
  • Mica sheets and insulation layers only provide protection for convection (and radiation). Although they are usually placed on the modules/HV BBs to protect them from external arcing. Also, the MFR profile used here is a very relaxed scenario (there are cases 5 times bigger).
  • The time it takes for the first cell of the NEM to trigger the short-circuit phase is 5.4 min (without coolant). If static coolant is added, this time is delayed by 1 min, and by 3 min if it is flowing @ 5 lpm. However, the NEM does not go into TR if the coolant is flowing @ 10 lpm (at the inlet). Although in some TP tests coolant is not present at all.
  • Adding static coolant didn't change the cell-to-cell (C2C) TP time in the EM, but if it is flowing, it is delayed by 1 min. It can be further greatly improved by using insulation layers (e.g. aerogels) between the cells and by directing the gas towards the top cover, which may also reduce the risk of short circuit inside the module.

Battery, module & cold plate

It is possible to account for the heat generated due to the current in the internal (C2C) busbars. However, because of the small length and cross-sectional area of these busbars, the amount of heat generated is relatively small. For this case, I designed a simplified battery module that consists of the following:

  • Jellyrolls (12S2P configuration)
  • Casings
  • Internal/C2C BBs
  • External BBs
  • TIM
  • Cold plate
  • Aerogels

The module has a cold/cooling plate where coolant is flowing @ 10 lpm (through the inlet), and each cell releases around 60 W of heat. In this case there is no convective boundary condition to take into account the effect of the ambient. Thus, the ohmic heat generated by the 120 A current didn't reach a steady state.


It is possible to measure the volumetric flow rate (VFR) through each channel of the cold plate to see how balanced the flow is. The highest difference was for channel 4 that was 25.6% above the target VFR, while the lowest was for channels 1, 2, 7 and 8, which were around 7.8% below the target. Additionally, the pressure drop for this case was around 6.5 kPa. The main reason for this high PD is the length of the channels.


The temperature distribution of the cells is shown in the two figures above. The average heat transfer coefficient (HTC) for this case is around 1530 W/m2K, and the maximum temperature difference within each cell is almost the same for all of them, with an average of 11.4ÂºC. Cells 21 and 22 have the highest average temperature, and not the last two, since those are exposed to a greater surface area of coolant. The trend of the temperature of the cells is better seen in the immersion cooling section.


Battery, immersion cooling

To compare the performance between a cold plate and immersion cooling, I made slight modifications to the previous design. I replaced the aerogels with small seperators, which are placed every two cells, and I let the dielectric fluid flow into the module from the bottom part of the end plate and leave the module from the top part. The inputs are the same as the previous case, with the only difference of the coolant being a dielectric fluid.



The temperature distribution in the cells is largely improved by using immersion cooling, as seen in the two figures above. It is observed that the last two cells have the highest temperatures, but this can be easily improved by allowing more coolant flow towards those cells, and restricting the flow from going towards the first two cells. The pressure drop in this case is almost the same as using the cold plate of the previous case.


The left figure shows the minimum, maximum and average temperature of every cell, when using a cold plate and immersion cooling. It is clear that the temperature gradient is greatly reduced when using the latter approach.

By using immersion cooling, the average of the maximum temperature difference of all cells is around 2ÂºC, which is an 82% reduction compared to using a standard cold plate.

2. 2D Kelvin-Helmholtz instability

  • Kelvin-Helmholtz instability can appear as a solution to the Euler equations (inviscid form of the N-S eqs.) with the condition of having a velocity difference across an interface.
  • To solve these equations I used the predictor-corrector scheme as shown in the right figure. Numerically, I set up the instability with a random perturbation injected to the initial velocity field.
  • The above animation represents the density field, with periodic boundary conditions set at the left and right sides, while outflow conditons are assumed at the top and bottom of the domain.

3. Parallel solution of the 2D heat equation

  • To solve the heat propagation throughout a rectangular plate (above simulation), I used the Alternating Direction Implicit (ADI) scheme, which allows us to solve a tridiagonal matrix instead of a pentadiagonal one.
  • To implement the domain decomposition method (i.e. like a MPI parallelization), I applied the ADI scheme to each subdomain, so that they are solved in a parallel manner and the communication is taken place at the interface, where I imposed a Neumann/Dirichlet boundary condition.
  • The heat flux q_m is computed with information of subdomain 2 and the temperature T_m is taken from the profile of subdomain 1.
  • Additionally, each subdomain is solved in a parallel manner using OpenMP.
